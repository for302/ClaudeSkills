Introduction 

Human Performance Technology (HPT) also referred to as Human Performance Improvement (HPI) is the result of people working together within a system to produce maximum performance. It consists of problem-based analysis with the analysis of data from observation, cost-effectiveness, feedback, and communication for achieving set targets and accomplishing goals. Additionally, human performance technology concentrates on individual behaviors and activities rather than just performance to resolve organizational performance problems. Over years, the field of HPT has developed a wide range of methods and theories applicable for a comprehensive set of situations for organizations to leverage performance. 

History

In the 19th century, Joseph Lancaster of England was the first to design a “monitorial method for teaching a large number of students” (Chyung, 2008).  However, the notion of human performance technology is widely acknowledged and associated with being birthed during the 1960s, a  behaviorism error, led by a graduate student by the name of Thomas. F. Gilbert that was studying directly under B. F. Skinner (Reiser & Dempsey, 2018).  Gilbert's main focus, as it relates within the realm of behaviorism, resides within programmed instruction which was an expansion of Skinner's research on “teaching machines” (Reiser & Dempsey, 2018).  Throughout the next few decades, Gilbert pioneers with other learning research enthusiasts to form The National Society for Programmed Instruction.  Twenty years later Gilbert expands his research, developing six key environmental and individual predictors that impact on workplace performance (Reiser & Dempsey, 2018).  Before this, programmed instructional training, theorized, developed, implemented by those within the military.  Just as Skinner research directly influenced Gilbert, the same can be said for those who studied under Gilbert.  In fact, one of Gilberts students, Joe Harless, went on to develop a widely known model, Performance Improvement Process (Reiser & Dempsey, 2018).  It was also during this error where the birth of cognitive research was gaining rapid popularity for addressing the opposite behavioral concerns within instructional learning, focusing on how the mind learns, processes, stores, and retrieved information.  The field of instructional technology began to acknowledge that “instruction is not a cure-all…other interventions have to be utilized, and the focus has to be on performance improvement” which revolutionized the field of instructional performance because the focus shifted from being “behavior focused to performance-focused” evolving into the field as we now know it to be, Human Performance Technology (Chyung, 2018).  Human Performance Technology is more than a systemic or systematic approach to analyzing, design, providing and implementing solutions to address organizational performance-based concerns.  Human Performance Technology has more of a present “Design Thinkers” mindset where being empathic is essential for the successful creation of multiple solutions targeted to achieve desired performance results by way of and through your number one asset, your employees.

Definition 

“Human Performance Technology is  a systematic approach to improving productivity and competence.” \- (ISPI, 2002\)

The Association for Talent Development defines Performance Improvement as “the process of identifying and analyzing important organizational and individual performance gaps, planning for the future performance improvement, designing and developing cost-effective and ethically justifiable interventions to close performance gaps, implementing the interventions, and evaluating the financial and non-financial results.” 

It is a problem-solving suite of methods, procedures, and strategies and thereby designing the right kind of interventions to measure and analyze human performance, outcomes, behaviors, and accomplishments. In simple words, HPT includes methods and strategies to reduce the gap between the actual and the desired performance within an organization. 

Action 

The HPT model provides a detailed and systematic process for examining organizational performance problems. One must first understand the goals of the organization as well as the goals of the initiative that seeks to improve the performance concern. The performance gap is the difference between the current performance level and the desired performance level. Identifying the performance gap allows one to see which areas can be improved. This is the starting point for identifying problems that allow root causes to be recognized and the proper interventions to be implemented. The HPT process for performance analysis has been applied to many different fields including healthcare, financial, construction, and transportation. The success of HPT across various industries can be credited to the transferability of the organizational environment, current and desired workplace performance, ­­potential causes, and implemented interventions analyses to many different situations. In Rothwell, a forklift operation used HPT methodology to identify why forklift accidents were occurring at a frequency higher than the organization viewed as acceptable. The process involved the review of accident reports, training material, and national safety and health information on forklifts to determine the causes of the problem which included lack of forklift knowledge, work environment issues, and safety policy gaps. These efforts led to the implementation of five interventions designed to help to decrease the frequency of accidents. It also led to the development of a forklift safety program that has been sold to other companies for decades. Rothwell also speaks of a state’s Department of Transportation that found success with the model. A state’s Department of Transportation (DOT) enlisted the help of a few HPI consultants to ensure their projects were completed within budget, on time, and within government guidelines. The consultant’s focused on the site inspector role because that role held the responsibility of ensuring that all DOT work sites were safe and abided by environmental regulations. A careful performance analysis showed that the inspectors were tasked with many administrative duties, dealt with poor technology such as internet connectivity, and lacked skills such as computer literacy and blueprint reading. These were all factors that deterred the inspectors from properly inspecting the worksites. Based on the performance and causal analysis, three interventions were proposed that were designed to help the site inspectors do their job more effectively.

 

Trends 

Conclusion 

Over the past four decades, the field of HPT has developed and employed many theories and applications to resolve performance-based problems. It results in higher profits, greater customer retention, aligned organizations that work more effectively across functions and across levels, cost leveraging not just cost savings, and companies that can change as fast as their market, and faster than their competition. HPT will continue to evolve as the need to improve performance is being felt across all the industries and society at large. New standards will be developed to meet these performance improvement needs in evolving organizations that best adapt to new practices and HPT trends. 

Similarly, HPT in learning and performance is creating an increase in technology-based learning, video conferencing, broadcasts, just-in-time learning, simulations, scenario-based learning, micro-learning etc. Social learning and networking also contribute to learning causes within an organization, thereby improving performance. HPI practitioners use a variety of techniques to determine the right kind of learning intervention by studying the specific circumstances and challenges within an organization. 

Human Performance Technology is all about helping individuals and organizations to create greater value for themselves and their stakeholders.
